Chess.

A Timeless Game

Setup.

The Gateway to Mastery

To get started, you'll need a chess board and a friend (optional).

In chess, players alternate turns, the player with the white pieces always starting first. This continues until a player wins or they draw. To begin, set up your the board as shown in the image. draw.

We'll see how games can end in more detail later. For now, let's explore how each piece moves.

The Pawn.

Small, Yet Mighty

The pawn is the most basic chess piece. Each pawn can move forward one square at a time, except on its very first move where it has the option to move two squares forward. Pawns may capture enemy pieces directly diagonally in front of them, taking their place.

When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it is promoted to any other piece (except a king), usually a queen.

The Knight.

The Trickster of the Board

A knight moves in an L-shape: two squares in one direction (either horizontally or vertically) and then one square perpendicular, or vice versa. It captures enemy pieces by landing on their square.

Another unique property about the knight is that it is the only piece that can jump over other pieces.

The Rook.

Strength in Simplicity

A rook can move any number of squares along a row or column, but it cannot jump over other pieces. It can capture an enemy piece by moving into its square.

Along with the king, a rook can participate in castling, a special move that improves the king's safety. We'll show how to do this in a later section.

The Bishop.

Long-Range Diagonal Mastery

A bishop can move any number of squares diagonally, but it cannot jump over other pieces. It captures an enemy piece by moving into its square.

Notice that it is restricted to the color of the square it starts on (either light or dark) for the entire game, as no diagonal path can cross between the two colors.

The Heart of the Game

The king can also perform castling with a rook, moving two squares towards the rook, while the rook moves to the square the king passed through.

The game ends in checkmate if the king is unable to escape a threat of capture.

Checkmate.

Nowhere to go

We say a king is in check when it is threatened by an enemy piece. When this happens, the player must make a move to escape the threat: either move the king, capture the threatening piece, or block the threat with another piece. An extension of this rule is that no player may "put themselves in check" because it would allow the other player to capture their king on their turn.

If a player's king is in check and there is no legal move to get out of check, the game is over. This is called checkmate. In several of the previous puzzles, the winning move checkmated the opponent's king. Try to find the move that checkmates the black king in the puzzle on the left.
